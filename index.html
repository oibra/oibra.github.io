forager

as a project for my human-computer interaction class, my group designed and prototyped an app called forager. with the explosion of popularity foraging has gained in recent years, we wanted to make the entire process simpler and lower the barrier of entry for new foragers. forager gives mushroom foragers a smoother and more fulfilling experience. you can read more in our blog post. this project was awarded "overall best design" in our human-computer interaction course.

check it out!

intro java style linter

an interactive information web app, created for the Client-Side Web Development course in the iSchool at the university of washington. This web app functions as an educational Java linter that checks the style of users' input Java code and inform users of what the style errors are with suggestions on how to fix them. This app is built around the style guidelines for CSE 14X (Intro to computer programming 1 & 2 at the university of washington) with those students as the primary audience, but should be a helpful resource for any novice Java learners.

readycipe

Readycipe was created as a final project for my intro to informatics class to streamline the food-making process and make cooking more accessible for lower-income people working on tighter budgets with less free time for shopping and cooking. We created a prototype of a machine that would facilitate the streamlining of the food-preparation process for users. I created the UI for the machine and the basics of a backend.

note: this project was developed with certain hardware in mind, and it's only made to display well on a portrait computer screen. sorry mobile users!

check it out!

SNAPtrack

SNAPTrack is an app i helped develop for the dubhacks 2018 hackathon. we created it with the intention to help people who use SNAP benefits by providing them with information to help them make the most of their SNAP benefits. it's taking the place of a now defunct service that informed people about what they could use SNAP benfits on, while also providing more specific services; store and item specific information about where they can use their benefits, what they can use their benefits for, and scraping user receipt data to better flesh out the database of SNAP eligible items/places and locating stores where users can use SNAP benefits. i created the front-end of the project.

sigcse 2021 post-mortem

04.10.2021

SIGCSE 2021 was the first, and, as I move ever closer to graduating this year, seemingly the last academic conference of my college career. I’ve heard faculty members talk about all the issues with the conference, and I get where they’re coming from, but for me, and maybe because it was my first conference, it was amazing; it was a lifeline. I started this year full of passion for CS Ed and applied to 7 PhD programs to study it after I graduated. When SIGCSE started, I had been rejected from 5 of those programs, and I would know I was rejected from the other 2 by the time SIGCSE was over. Suffice to say, I was feeling pretty convinced that CS Ed just might not be for me. SIGCSE completely pulled me out of that. A full week of meeting droves of people passionate about CS education, getting to experience nuanced, deep, passionate discussions about things I’ve never had people to talk with about, getting to hear people talk about years and years of experience with things I’d only ever heard about. It was enlightening; like finding a new world full of people who care just as much about this niche thing I care about as me. For people already acquainted with that world, I’m sure the virtual conference didn’t deliver all the networking, connecting, and reuniting it normally does. But for someone discovering that world for the first time, especially with social anxiety, the whole experience was revelatory and refreshing. It was also bittersweet.

SIGCSE 2021 was the first conference of my college career, but it wasn’t supposed to be. Just a year before, I had been packing for SIGCSE 2020, which my department had funded me to attend. The conference, which was set to be in nearby Portland OR, was cancelled last minute due to COVID-19, and I never got to go. As I experienced SIGCSe for the first time this year, as excited as I was, I couldn’t help but wonder what it would’ve been like to be able to attend earlier in my college career. How might my path have been different -- how might I have been different, if I had been able to experience everything I was experiencing now a year earlier, long before I jumped into graduate program applications without a clue as to what I was doing or where I was going. I am truly happy I got to experience SIGCSE before I graduated, but God do I wish I could’ve had some of those formative experiences just a little sooner.

On a brighter note, SIGCSE also left me with some hope for my future with CS education; when it started, I was pretty much ready to give up on it. But in my time attending the conference, I met so many people, who took so many paths to get into the field I thought I was ready to give up on. It was really nice to see living, breathing proof that just because the path I thought I would take didn’t work out, I shouldn’t give in to despair just yet.
